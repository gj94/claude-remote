# House of Leaves — Chapter III Recap

**Epigraph:** "It is no accident that the photographer becomes a photographer any more than the lion tamer becomes a lion tamer." — Dorothea Lange

Secondary epigraph: Exodus 3:11 — Moses to God: *"But who am I that I should go to Pharaoh and free the Israelites?"* (Editor's translation in FN 24)

---

## What This Chapter Is

Chapter III asks a deceptively simple question: **"Why Navidson?"** — and answers it by excavating his psychology and background. It argues that Navidson was *destined* to enter that house and go as deep as he did.

---

## The Central Question

> *Why Navidson? Why not someone else?*

Zampanô frames this in terms of Dante asking Virgil why *he* has been chosen to descend into hell — comparing Navidson to a great explorer forced into something without divine guidance or mandate.

---

## The House's History

- Built in **1720**
- Has had approximately **0.37 owners per year** — a remarkably high turnover rate
- Nearly every occupant was *traumatized in some way*
- The house's malevolence (if it can be called that) **predates Navidson** — it cannot be a projection of his psychology because too many people before him suffered there
- Real estate agent: **Alicia Rosenbaum** — she eventually revealed the house's troubled ownership history

---

## Navidson's Background — Why He Was the One

### His Parents
- **Father** (Tony Navidson): St. Louis electronics salesman; alcoholic; prone to violent outbursts (threw a pan of pork chops at walls, smashed a windshield with a thermos); disappeared for weeks at a time without warning; died of **congestive heart failure**
- **Mother**: Aspired to be an actress; left the family to pursue producers; last seen in a Los Angeles bar, talking about moonlight and Hollywood; **vanished completely** — neither Will nor Tom ever heard from her again

### Psychological Damage (direct quotes from Dr. Rita Mistopolis)
> *"If love is minimal and abuse high, the damage will be permanent and the children will suffer emotional impairments for the rest of their lives."*

Both Will and Tom learned to treat anything good in their lives as **temporary**. Accustomed to discontinuity, abandonment, emotional instability.

### Why Photography?
- Photography gave Navidson *permanence for fleeting moments*
- But **"not even ten thousand photographs can secure a world"** — his labor couldn't replace the love and security he never had
- Photography was also a way to stay in motion, keep distance — from Karen, from commitment, from himself

### The Word "Outpost"
- Navidson describes his intention: *"I just want to create a cozy little outpost for me and my family."*
- Zampanô zeroes in on **"outpost"** — by definition, a base set up to *protect against hostile forces outside*
- This reveals Navidson's true psychology: he sees the world as hostile and wanted to use images/documentation to build a fortress against loss
- Abandoning photography = surrendering to loss — that's why he couldn't quit

---

## Will and Tom — The Twin Dynamic

- **Fraternal twins** (not identical)
- Haven't spoken in over **8 years** by the time the story begins
- Tom is content, earthy — watches sports, gets by; carries deep pools of sorrow in his eyes
- Will drives himself, achieves, obsesses — also carries those same deep eyes
- Zampanô: *"Like Tom, Navidson's eyes share the same water."*
- Their estrangement has never been fully explained to the audience

## Johnny Truant's Footnotes — Key Content

### Who is Lude (more detail)?
- Real name possibly **Harry** — no one calls him that
- Hollywood native through and through; hair stylist; always surrounded by beautiful women
- He and Johnny wander the city's late hours together; Lude wants money, parties, women; Johnny wants *"something else. I'm not even sure what to call it... roomy and it's drenched in sunlight and it's weightless..."*

### Johnny's Own Background (First Real Revelation)
> *"As a counselor once told me — a Counselor For Disaffected Youth: 'You like that crap because it reminds you of you.'"*

- Father died when Johnny was **10**
- Mother died ~9 years later — described as *"crazy Shakespearean"*
- He grew up as a ward of the state / under the care of others; went to work in Alaska at 13; by 18 had *"already slept in a whorehouse in Rome"*

### The Trunk (March 1998 — 3 months after finding the manuscript)
- **Over 200 rejection letters** from literary journals and publishers found in the trunk — no one wanted Zampanô's work
- Johnny describes the trunk as *"an old cedar lined jobber, built in Utica, NY, special thanks to the C. M. Clapp Company, complete with rusted latches, rotting leather handles"*
- He started putting the pieces together reluctantly; now can't stop

## Final Answer to "Why Navidson?"

> *"Considering the practically preadamite history of the house, it was inevitable someone like Navidson would eventually enter those rooms."*

> *"Considering his own history, talent and emotional background, only Navidson could have gone as deep as he did and still have successfully brought that vision back."*

---

## Key Themes

- **Nature vs. nurture in trauma**: The house exists independently of Navidson's psychology — yet his psychology shapes *how* he responds to it
- **Photography as armor**: Making images = staying in control; entering the house = surrendering control
- **Twin doubling**: Will and Tom are mirrors of each other — success vs. failure, engagement vs. withdrawal; both scarred by the same origin
- **Inherited darkness**: Both Navidson and Johnny were shaped by absent/destructive parents

---

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

- Navidson's mother said she wanted to *"bring down the house"* — literal and metaphorical resonance with what follows
- Navidson's twin was with him during "The Five and a Half Minute Hallway" — their estrangement resolves (temporarily) *because* of the house
- The house's 0.37 owners/year — what happened to all of them? The book never fully catalogs this
- Note footnote 31: Zampanô himself published this chapter as "The Matter of Why" in *LA Weekly*, May 19, 1994 — meaning he had readers and some public profile despite obscurity
- Johnny parallels Navidson closely: both lost parents, both carry private traumas, both drawn to danger they can't explain
