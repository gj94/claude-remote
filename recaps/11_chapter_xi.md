# House of Leaves — Chapter XI Recap

**Epigraph:** Charles Baudelaire (French + translated) — *"The poet, sick, and with his chest half bare / Tramples a manuscript in his dark stall, / Gazing with terror at the yawning stair / Down which his spirit finally must fall."*

Footnote 220 is Zampanô's placeholder: *"Something about the terror of the staircase."* — He never filled it in. The Editors supplied the translation.

---

## What This Chapter Is

Chapter XI belongs almost entirely to **Tom**. While Navidson and Reston are deep in the labyrinth rescuing Wax and Jed, Tom is alone at the relay camp near the top of the Spiral Staircase — two nights in no man's land, his Hi 8 rolling, keeping himself sane with bad jokes, hand shadows, radio calls, and weed. Interwoven with his transcript is a severely fragmented academic analysis of Tom and Will as Esau and Jacob — an essay Zampanô apparently destroyed in a fit of rage, leaving only bloodstained scraps. The chapter ends with Tom attempting to descend the staircase, the growl returning, the stairs physically stretching beneath him, and Tom sprinting back up to safety.

The structural device throughout: **rzzzzzzzzzzzzzzzzzz** — lines of static separating every fragment, mimicking a signal breaking apart.

---

## Tom's Character Profile

Zampanô gives a full portrait of Tom Navidson here, framing him against his twin:

- **Will:** fierce ambition, compulsion for risk, relied on aggression to anchor the world
- **Tom:** passive, accepting, no awards, no fame, no job held more than a year or two, no relationship longer than a few months, no city for longer than a few years. *"He drifted, bending to daily pressures."*
- Despite this: Tom is **better liked** than Will. Soft, easy-going, a peacefulness *"typically reserved for Buddhist monks."*
- Anne Kligman's essay on Tom condensed 53 interviews — all speak warmly of a man they *"did not know all that well but nonetheless valued and in some cases appeared to genuinely love."*
- Tom is **a recovering alcoholic** — 12-step program, inspired admirers. Dulls ongoing pain with alcohol and *"a few joints a day"* — his *"friendly haze."*

### The Estrangement
For **eight years** Tom and Will were estranged — *"when the old rug was pulled out from under old Tom."* During those years: chemical dependency, unemployment, ended a relationship with a schoolteacher. The likely cause: **Chad's birth**. The year Chad turned one was the year Tom *"felt orphaned at forty."* Will's attention had shifted from his brother to his son.

### Tom as Peacemaker
Once back in Will's orbit, Tom consistently reduced tension between Navidson and Karen. Academic reading: Tom's desire to *"reacquire his lost parental figures"* transmuted Navidson into a father figure and Karen into a mother figure. Nam Eurtton's counter-reading: *"Why? Because Tom's a nice guy."*

## The Esau/Jacob Fragment

Zampanô wrote an extended scholarly analysis comparing Tom and Will to Jacob and Esau. He then **tore it to shreds and apparently tried to flush it**, leaving pages bloodstained in a wastebasket. His scribe **Denise Neiman** (now married, living in Tel Aviv) rescued the scraps and stored them in a manila envelope in the chest where Johnny found the manuscript.

Johnny transcribed what could be read. It is barely coherent — fragments separated by rzzzzzz static lines:

### The Academic Content (fragments)
- **Ruccalla's reading**: Will = Jacob (smooth, cunning intellectual, claiming birthright through his camera/mask), Tom = Esau (unkempt, lumbering, giving things up)
- **Counter-reading (Nam Eurtton)**: Will = Esau (a hunter who shoots with his camera), Tom = Jacob (Zen-calm)
- **Freed Kashon's objection**: Actually *Holloway* is Esau — the beard, the hunter's profession, the tension with Navidson mirrors Jacob vs. brother better than Tom does
- **Etymology**: Esau from *ash* = "to hurry"; Jacob from *akav* = "to delay/restrain." Also: Esau = "to cover" (hair); Jacob = "heel" (born clutching Esau's heel)
- **Key quote (Ruccalla's conclusion)**: *"Like the serpents of the Caduceus, these two brothers have always been and always will be inextricably intertwined; and just like the Caduceus, their shared history creates a meaning and that meaning is health."*
- **Zampanô's note, surviving**: *"There never having been a blessing to begin with... No birthright, all of it a misleading ploy, both brothers fools."*

### Why Zampanô Destroyed It
Johnny speculates: *"Maybe it really was too personal. Maybe he had a brother. A son. Maybe he had two sons."* The blood on the pages suggests the act of tearing was violent. Denise Neiman found his fingers were bandaged after — he claimed he'd fallen.

## Tom's Story — Transcript

Tom films himself on Hi 8 for roughly two and a half days. What follows is the shape of that recording:

### Day 1
- Talks directly to *"Mr. Monster"* — an imagined creature he invents to have something to address in the dark. Half-joking, half-genuinely terrified.
- Radio check with Navidson: *"See any ghosts?"* Navidson: *"You a little spooked?"* Tom: *"Lighting up a fat one."*
- Radio check with Karen: *"I'm cold, I'm scared shitless, and I feel like I'm about to be eaten alive at any moment. Otherwise, I'd say I'm fine."*
- Tells Mr. Monster he won't taste good — years of fast food, black lungs from smoking.
- Vomits at 21:09. *"This is not fun. This isn't fair."* — then: *"I think there's a game on tonight."*
- Night: Karen relays that Daisy and Chad heard **voices** in the house. Neither could understand what they were saying.
- Closes with: *"Tell him I love him, Tom."* / *"I already did."*

### Day 2
- More jokes. The **red shirt / brown pants** joke. The **punker on the bus** joke. The **celebrating monk** joke (the original word in the bible was *"celebrate"* not *"celibate"* — *"Oh Lord Jesus. The word is 'celebrate.'"*). The **seven dwarves** / Pope / penguin joke.
- Asks Karen over radio to send a pizza delivery boy *"two miles down on the left."*
- Tells Mr. Monster the story of *"Love, Death & Nike"*: poor man given Nikes, loses his calluses, feet get infected, legs cut off, dies. Tom's reading: kindness can strip away your defenses and leave you worse off.
- **Hand shadows**: Tom uses his halogen lamp to cast a whole menagerie on the tent wall — frog, piglet, elephant, woodpecker, snail, praying mantis, sea urchin, dove, tiger, rabbit — and finally a dragon. *"Mr. Monster."* He turns off the lamp. Black.
  > *Footnote 249: "All of them are as mutable as letters, as permanent as fame."* — The creatures only exist in light. When the light dies, nothing survives.
- **Karen's crisis**: her **Feng Shui objects** — crystals, bullfrogs, goldfish, dragons — have **all disappeared** from the house. Not her, not Daisy, not Chad. *"Tom, they're the ones who told me. They wanted to know why I'd gotten rid of it all."* Karen is crying. *"After this, I'm leaving. I'm taking the kids and I'm going."*
- Tom gently redirects her. Radio to Navidson: *"Not so good, Navy. She's pretty scared. You should get back here."* — signal fades to static.
- Nighttime ranting at Mr. Monster: *"I've been shitting in your corner. I've been pissing on your wall."* Then notes the house consumes even that — the waste just vanishes.

### Day 3: The Descent Attempt
- Radio fragment at 00:54: *"Jed's been shot, he's bleed—"* [static]. Tom: *"Shot? By who?!"* Then the sounds of the gunfight (pop-pop-pop, cracks, Reston's *"awwwwww shit"*) bleed through the static. Tom can't make out what's happening.
- Later: Navidson tells Tom to **meet them at the bottom of the stairs** — they need help carrying Wax. Estimates 8 hours.
- Dawn. Tom drops a lightstick down the staircase. It flickers and dies immediately.

> *"No way. Not gonna happen, Navy. I've been alone in this shithole for almost three days and now you want me to go down there alone?"*

He tries anyway. Makes it down the first flight. Then the second flight. Then:

> **The stairs suddenly stretch and drop ten feet. The circular stairwell bends into an ellipse, then snaps back.**

Then the **growl** — *"More like a roar. Almost deafening. As if it originated right next to Tom."*

Tom sprints back up, camera a blur. Reaches the top. Immediately radios Karen:

> **"I'm coming in."**

Tom abandons his relay post and retreats into the main house.

## Zampanô's Coda: "A Short Analysis of Tom's Story"

Navidson edited Tom's Story **months after** the events, knowing how Tom's story ends. Zampanô reads the editing as *"a labor of love"* — clean cuts, beautiful sound balance, intense attention to ordinary moments.

> *"Perhaps because Tom's antics are so amusing and so completely permeated with warmth, we could easily miss how hand shadows, an abundance of bad jokes and the birth of 'Mr. Monster' ultimately come to mean Sorrow."*

Navidson saw — in retrospect — what he had failed to see for years: his brother's warmth, courage, and love. The editing is grief made into craft.

> *"As if Navidson with his great eye had for the first time seen what over the years he never should have missed. Or should have missed all along."*

The ambiguity of that final line is intentional and devastating.

## Johnny Truant — Footnote 249: The Pekinese

The longest Johnny footnote in the chapter. Set the **December before** he found Zampanô's manuscript — the aftermath of the Ecstasy-soaked November described in Chapter X.

### Lude's List
Lude compiled a month-long tally of sexual encounters for November — 20+ women. Johnny wrote it down, apparently finding it funny at the time. The list is printed in full.

Then Johnny revisited the same list later, and **pencilled in what he now knew** about each woman:

- Monique — husband recently left her
- Kelly — mother forced her to perform oral sex at age eleven
- Caroline — first abortion at age twelve
- Mann — uncle would come over and finger her
- Alicia — lost her virginity to her father
- Leslie — raped by gym coach at fourteen
- Dawn — date raped last year
- Melissa — ex-boyfriend used to hit her; had to get a nose job
- Dana — prostitute
- Susan — hole in the roof of her mouth from too much cocaine

> *"Scratch in your own guesses. No doubt your postils will be happier than mine, though if they are, you clearly don't know what the fuck you're talking about."*

Johnny's three encounters that November: Gabriella (body covered in a birthmark, gorgeous and gentle, left for Milan the next day), Barbara (couldn't remember his name by morning), and **Clara English** — who told him about a rape when he asked too many questions. She turned on him for knowing. He still thinks about her.

> *"Can Clara English ever recover or is she permanently wounded, damned to stagger under years devoid of meaning & love until finally the day comes when she stumbles and is swept away?"*

### The Pekinese
End of November, running out of energy, Lude drags Johnny to the Opium Den. There he meets **Johnnie** (also called Sled; real name Rachel) — described as artificially perfect, surgically altered, *"probably closer to six thousand years old."* Something is wrong with her but he can't name it.

On the way to her truck, they find a stray **Pekinese** — dirty, trembling, no tags, snot pouring from its nose. Johnny picks it up. Despite wanting to keep it, Johnnie insists she has a yard, a home in the valley, a *"happy pet land"* — and takes the dog. She drives Johnny home. He doesn't invite her in. Something feels off.

As her truck pulls away, he hears a **thump**. Jogs down the block. Finds the Pekinese on the pavement — **half its head caved in, an eye broken and oozing, tongue caught in its snapped jaws.** She'd thrown it out the window as she drove.

> *"I tried to picture those claw-like hands grabbing this poor creature by the neck and hurling it out her window. Had she even looked at what she held? Had she even glanced back?"*

Johnny crouches over the body. Can't cry. Can't speak. Can't decide what to do with it. Tries to give it a name — lists run through his mind and run out. Too late. He stands up and leaves.

> *"Call me an asshole (and fuck you too) my Pekinese friend was gone. Ant food now."*

The Pekinese is the story of the wounded and where they end up. A projection of Johnny himself — and of every woman on Lude's list.

### Outburst at the Tattoo Shop
Triggered by the Tom hand-shadow passage, Johnny had an outburst at work when a biker talked about the *permanence* of ink. Johnny started shouting — *"Permanent? Are you fucking loopy, man?"* — spitting, snot, real volume. Thumper escorted him out: *"I don't know what you're getting messed up in Johnny but it's fucking you up bad."* She touched his arm and he wanted to tell her everything. He choked it down.

## Key Themes

- **Tom as the book's secret heart**: The chapter re-frames Tom as the most emotionally genuine character — someone who has accepted limitation without bitterness and still manages warmth, humor, and love. Will only recognizes this in retrospect.
- **The relay that fails**: Tom was meant to maintain radio contact and meet them at the stairs. The house defeats both functions — static destroys the signal, the growing staircase defeats the meeting. Tom's role collapses.
- **The house consumes everything**: Feng Shui objects, waste, light, signals, distance markers — the house absorbs and erases. Nothing leaves a mark.
- **Lude's List Revisited**: A structural gut-punch. The November of fun was actually a November of walking through the wreckage of women's lives without seeing it. Johnny sees it now. The drugs that erased consequences also erased the ability to see other people.
- **The Pekinese as emblem**: What happens to the wounded. The creature found, briefly given hope, then destroyed by someone who claimed to be its rescue. Johnny couldn't save it. Can't save himself either.
- **Sorrow as editorial act**: Navidson's editing of Tom's Story is the book's first explicit act of mourning. We don't yet know what we're mourning, but we know something has been lost.

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

| Item | Note |
|------|------|
| Tom abandons relay post | He was Navidson and Reston's radio link and the plan to meet them at the bottom of the stairs. He retreated before they arrived. What happens when they reach the staircase and Tom isn't there? |
| The growl returned | The house growled at Tom on the staircase — louder than before, *"almost deafening."* It had been dormant during Navidson and Reston's descent. It came back for Tom. |
| Stairs stretched for Tom | The staircase expanded when Tom tried to descend — the opposite of what happened for Navidson (it shrank). Haugeland's theory: Tom's *fear and ignorance* expanded it; Navidson's *knowledge* contracted it. |
| Feng Shui objects gone | Karen's objects vanished from the house — children didn't take them, Karen didn't take them. The house consumed them. This is the first instance of the house directly affecting the main living space (not the hallway). |
| Children heard voices | Daisy and Chad heard *several voices* in the house they couldn't understand — before the shooting. Another instance of the house's effect reaching the family. |
| Zampanô's destroyed pages | His personal breakdown over the Esau/Jacob material — *"There never having been a blessing to begin with"* — suggests his own family history mirrors the twins' dynamic. His bandaged fingers suggest the destruction was violent. |
| *"Should have missed all along"* | Final line of chapter is deliberately ambiguous: Navidson saw what he *never should have missed* (Tom's value) OR *should have missed all along* (Tom's death — i.e., he never should have brought him into the house). Both readings are valid. |
| Editors' emails | Three reader emails appended to Lude's List: Clarissa (he was a *"shitty fuck"*), Natalie (misses him; asks about Johnny; asks whose blood was on his hands in the Introduction), Bethami (*"it takes two to tango"*).
