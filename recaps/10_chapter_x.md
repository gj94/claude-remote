# House of Leaves — Chapter X Recap

**Epigraph:** Dagobert Frey, *Grundlegung zu einer vergleichenden Kunstwissenschaft* — every house is an architecturally structured "path," pre-determining movement, and simultaneously a "goal" one advances toward or departs from.

---

## What This Chapter Is

Chapter X is the rescue chapter — Navidson, Tom, and Reston enter the house to find Holloway's team. It is also the chapter where the book's most disturbing violence occurs: Holloway shoots Jed. The chapter interweaves documentary action with a dense academic interlude on how the house mutates according to the psychology and *knowledge* of whoever enters it — a theory the chapter then demonstrates in real time.

---

## The Rescue Team Enters

Karen is left behind. She "loses herself in resentment and fear." Navidson by contrast is joyful, almost euphoric — as if having *any* purpose in that place is reason enough for joy.

- **Equipment**: 16mm motion picture (color and B/W) and 35mm stills — the most complete documentation Navidson has attempted inside.
- **Route**: Through the Anteroom → Great Hall → Spiral Staircase

Denise Lowery (*Sketches: The Process of Entry*, 1996) wrote a prose poem describing Navidson's photography of the Anteroom — flares catching on Tom's body, spokes of Reston's wheelchair, shadow-shapes on the walls, halogen lamps unable to penetrate more than a fraction of the space.

The **Great Hall** is nearly a mile in span — impossible to illuminate. Reston in foreground with flare, Tom surrounded by flares in the background near the Spiral Staircase — both swallowed by darkness. *"The inside of some preternatural hull designed to travel vast seas never before observed in this world."* (Chris Thayil, *National Geographic*)

## Tom Breaks Down at the Staircase

Tom refuses to descend. He is pale, sweating despite the cold, out of breath.

> **Navidson:** "Sorry Tom, I didn't mean to snap at you like that."
> **Tom:** [says nothing]
> **Navidson:** "Do you think you can stay here with Billy or do you want to head home?"
> **Tom:** "I'll stay here."
> **Reston:** "With Billy? The hell if you think I'm letting you go on alone."

But Navidson has already started down.

> **Reston (shouting after him):** "I should sue the bastards who designed this house. Haven't they heard of handicap ramps?"

---

## The Staircase Has Shrunk

Critical: Holloway's team took **nearly four days** to reach the bottom. Navidson reaches it in **under five minutes** — the staircase, which once measured an estimated 13 miles deep, is now only ~100 feet.

**Tom immediately assumes they're on the wrong staircase.** Navidson checks — neon trail markers from Holloway's team confirm it's the same one.

- **Reston** abandons his wheelchair and crawls down the stairs. Reaches the bottom in under 20 minutes.
- Navidson goes back up to retrieve the wheelchair and gear.
- **Tom seems fine** camping at the top. Both Navidson and Reston hope his position will maintain radio contact longer than Holloway's team could manage — though both know the house will eventually devour the signal.

## Key Academic Interlude: The House and Psychology

Zampanô stages an extended debate about whether the house's mutations reflect the psychology of its inhabitants.

### Ruby Dahi — "Solipsistic Heightener"
> *"The house, the halls, and the rooms all become the self — collapsing, expanding, tilting, closing, but always in perfect relation to the mental state of the individual."*

Implication: Holloway's creature came from Holloway's mind. The tiny room that trapped Wax reflected his exhaustion and despair. Navidson's fast descent reflected his *knowledge* that the staircase was finite.

### Dr. Haugeland — Epistemology Contracts Space
> *"Knowledge is hot water on wool. It shrinks time and space."*

The house feels vast the first time because it is unknown. On a second visit, familiarity dramatically contracts perceived distance. Like revisiting childhood places — they seem smaller, not because we are larger, but because we *know* them.

(Haugeland also promises a later chapter titled "Ennui" — never delivered, presumably part of the missing manuscript.)

### Norberg-Schulz Objects
Christian Norberg-Schulz insists architectural space exists independently of the perceiver. Hard to dispute with any civilization's buildings — but Navidson's house resists this assertion. The house exists for all, yet its size and layout shift differently for each person.

### Other voices
- **Günter Nitschke**: Space has "a centre which is perceiving man" — heterogeneous, finite, subjectively defined.
- **Kevin Lynch**: Environmental image is the product of immediate sensation *and* personal history.
- **Jean Piaget**: Perception of space involves gradual construction — it does not exist ready-made.

**Bottom line:** Whether the shifts are psychological or some undiscovered law of physics, Reston's nausea proves the house can have physiological consequences, not merely psychological ones.

## Inside the Labyrinth

As Navidson and Reston push into the labyrinth:

- Neon markers and fishing line — even **40 lb multi-strand steel line** — are being consumed by the house. "It looks like it's impossible to leave a lasting trace here."
- Reston suffers nausea — *mal de mer*, sea sickness — from the house's constant shifting. "Everything here is constantly shifting."
- Navidson's caution to Reston: if the staircase expands again on their way back, they will be stranded.

**Camp 1** (end of Day 1): Navidson removes and redresses the gauze around his infected toes (the stress-related skin condition from Chapter VII). Reston tinkers with his chair and the Arriflex mount. No sounds during the night.

**Camp 2** (end of Day 2 inside / Day 9 since Holloway set out): Both men are uncertain whether to continue or return. Then Navidson hears something — a voice or cry, confirmed by Reston.

---

## The Pursuit of the Voice

They leave most gear behind and chase the sound. For 40 minutes: nothing. Then another distant cry. Three more hours of weaving through rooms and corridors, neon arrows, fishing line.

- Navidson briefly gets Tom on the radio. **Tom tells him something is wrong with Karen.** Signal decays before details can be shared. (Karen's status left unresolved — carries forward.)

Reston identifies the sound's source: **the other side of a wall**. They find a corridor ending in a door → another corridor → another door → approximately **fifty doors** in a gauntlet, each opening onto the next.

**The last door has no doorknob.** And it is locked.

Navidson shoulders it four times. On the fourth: hinges tear free, bolt pops, door cracks to the floor.

---

## Jed Is Found — Then Shot

The camera captures Jed's face as the door falls — ashen, believing this is his final moment. Then recognition. Then laughter and sobs of relief.

> *"A thirty-three year old man from Vineland, New Jersey, who loves to drink Seattle coffee and listen to Lyle Lovett with his fiancée, learns his sentence has been remitted. He will live."*

A fraction of a second later:

> *One bullet pierced his upper lip, blasted through the maxillary bone, dislodging the central teeth (Reel 10; Frame 192), then in Frame 193 obliterated the back of his head — chunks of occipital lobe and parietal bone.*

The description is clinical, frame-by-frame — **deliberately modeled on Zapruder film analysis**. The text splinters typographically as the sequence describes the aftermath:

> *A life / üme / finished between / the space of / two frames.*

**Ken Burns** is quoted praising the sequence's realism — then a footnote reveals: *"Not only has Ken Burns never made any such comment, he's also never heard of The Navidson Record let alone Zampanô."* Zampanô fabricated the testimony.

## The Standoff

Reston returns fire with an **HK .45** he secretly brought.

> **Navidson:** "Since when did you bring a gun?"
> **Reston:** "Are you kidding me? This place is scary."

Using the Nikon with the Metz strobe (powerful flash), stills reveal the shooter: **Holloway**, standing in the middle of the corridor, rifle in hand.

As he lifts the weapon to fire at the flash — **the doors behind him begin slamming shut, one after another**. The house is acting. Holloway fires anyway; a bullet punches through a door. Then the last door slams, and silence fills the room.

**Holloway vanishes** behind the final closing door. No way to lock the first door from this side.

---

## Wax Alive, Jed Dying

- **Jed**: Still technically breathing but brain-dead. "It doesn't matter Rest. He's still dead."
- **Wax**: Alive, but in a fever-delirium, eyes fixed on a meaningless horizon — probably infected wound, onset of sepsis.

Navidson treats Wax:
- Elevates legs, cleans wound
- Half a tablet of meperidine (Demerol)

Reston converts the two-man tent into a **stretcher** using tent poles and pack straps — front end secured to his wheelchair.

> **Reston:** "What about Jed?"
> **Navidson:** "We'll leave his pack and mine behind."
> **Reston:** "Some habits die hard, huh?"
> **Navidson:** "Or they don't die." *(referenced to pp. 332–333 — not yet read)*

Navidson radios Tom — tells him to meet them at the bottom of the stairs.

## Johnny Truant — Footnote 211

The most extended Johnny footnote in the chapter. His psychological and physical deterioration is accelerating:

- **Physical symptoms**: mutilated inner cheeks (from grinding), aching teeth, aching head, stomach problems, unexplained screams in his sleep (reported by Bailey on the radio — see Ch. IX)
- **Saw Dr. Ogelmeyer**: Appointment arranged with a second doctor; prescribed a low-grade sedative. Cost: ~$175. Johnny tore up the appointment card.
- **Sold his possessions to pay for quiet**:
  - Sold radio/CD player to an Infiniti driver for $45
  - Sold CDs to Aaron's on Highland for ~$100
- **Laid out 10 chalk-blue sedative pills** on the kitchen counter. Refused to take them. Then **crushed them with his fingers and let the dust fall to the floor**.
- **Flushed** all alcohol, pot, seeds, and buds. Tossed tabs of old acid and some Ecstasy (hidden in a bag of rice).
- Notes past MDMA use: a month-long Ecstasy party (White Angels, Canaries, Stickmen, Snowballs, Hurricanes, Hallways, Butterflies, Tasmanian Devils, Mitsubishis) the November before Thanksgiving, 8 months prior.
- **Plans to buy a gun.** *"Then I'll crouch and I will wait."*
- Fireworks outside reveal the date: **July 4th**. He forgot his own birthday (which passed in Hailey's arms).
  > *"I can remember the beginnings of a nation that doesn't give a flying fuck about me... but I can't remember my own beginnings."*
- **Resolution**: *"I know what it means to go mad. I'll die before I go there."* He will stop blinking in the face of fear. He will hear what he screams. He will remember what he dreams.

## Key Themes

- **Knowledge as architecture**: The staircase shrank because Navidson *knew* it was finite. The house responds to epistemic states, not just psychological ones.
- **Traces that vanish**: Even steel fishing line is consumed by the house. No mark persists. The house erases history.
- **The hunter hunted**: Holloway, the professional hunter who arrived with a rifle, has become the predator within — shooting his own team. His weapon, introduced as a character detail in Chapter VII, becomes catastrophic.
- **Joy and death in the same second**: The structure of Jed's death is devastating precisely because his joy is confirmed an instant before it is erased. The book forces you to feel both emotions simultaneously.
- **Johnny's sobriety as parallel descent**: As Navidson goes deeper and strips away protection (gear, radio contact, light), Johnny strips away his chemical buffers. Both men are now going in unprotected.

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

| Item | Note |
|------|------|
| Staircase length | 13 miles for Holloway → 100 feet for Navidson. Confirmed same staircase by neon markers. This is not hallucination — it literally changed. |
| "Something wrong with Karen" | Tom told Navidson over a degrading radio. No details. Carries forward unresolved. |
| Holloway's location | He fired, retreated behind closing doors. Where he is now is unknown. He has a rifle. He is still inside the house. |
| Ken Burns quote | Fabricated by Zampanô — one of multiple "real-world" testimonials in the book that turn out to be invented. |
| "Or they don't die" | Navidson's cryptic response about habits; refers to pages 332–333, not yet reached. Likely connects to his war photojournalism instincts. |
| Reston's gun | He secretly brought an HK .45. He never mentioned it to Navidson. Acceptance of the impossible includes preparation for it. |
| Jed's details | His full humanity (Vineland NJ, coffee, Lyle Lovett, Texan fiancée) was established in Ch. VII precisely so his death lands here. Zampanô set this up 3 chapters in advance. |
| The doors slamming | The house acted during the standoff — slamming doors behind Holloway. Protective of Navidson and Reston? Expelling Holloway? Unknown. |
| Footnote 203 | Appears twice in the chapter text — likely a book production error, or intentional as another layer of the manuscript's deterioration. |
