# House of Leaves — Chapter II Recap

**Epigraph:** "The labours of men of genius, however erroneously directed, scarcely ever fail in ultimately turning to the solid advantage of mankind." — Mary Shelley

---

## What This Chapter Is

Chapter II zooms into the *opening* of The Navidson Record — the film Navidson intended to make vs. the one that emerged. It establishes the family, their dynamics, and the first signs of what is wrong beneath the surface of domestic bliss.

---

## Navidson's Original Intention

- He secured a **Guggenheim Fellowship** and an **NEA Media Arts Grant** for a completely benign project: documenting his family moving into a country house in Virginia.
- *"I just thought it would be nice to see how people move into a place and start to inhabit it. Settle in, maybe put down roots."*
- The camera setup: Hi 8 cameras with **motion detectors** mounted in every room (except the 3 bathrooms). Plus 2 x 16mm Arriflexes and 35mm cameras on hand. It's home-movie-ish but elevated by Navidson's exceptional eye.

---

## The Opening of the Film — April 1990

Navidson on the porch, drinking lemonade, watching the sunset. Pure pastoral optimism.

### The House
- Small, old-style heritage house in rural **Virginia**
- **Corner of Succoth and Ash Tree Lane**
- They're unrolling rugs, arranging furniture, replacing bulbs, hanging pictures (including one of Navidson's own prize-winning photographs)

### The Family

| Character | Age/Notes |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Will Navidson** | Slowly edging through his 40s (one source says 48); lean, attractive |
| **Karen Green** | 37; former Ford Agency model; gave up Milan fashion shoots and Venetian Masques to raise children |
| **Chad** | 8 years old; more serious, anxious about the move |
| **Daisy** | 5 years old; giggly, approves of the house, misses "Bloomydales" |

### Chad's Key Quote
Navidson interviews his kids. Chad misses the sound of traffic — it was his "evening lullaby." Now:
> *"Sometimes it's just silent… No sound at all."*
> *"Does that scare you?" "It's like something's waiting."*

This is presented as natural childhood anxiety — but in retrospect reads as foreshadowing.

---

## The Navidson-Karen Relationship

The real, hidden subject of Navidson's film. Key details:
- They are **not married** — long-term companions for over 11 years
- Navidson's constant overseas assignments caused severe alienation; Karen gave him an ultimatum: **give up photography or lose his family**
- He couldn't choose, so he compromised by *making reconciliation into a documentary subject* — turning their relationship into his project
- Karen was **decimated** by critics and tabloids after the film's release — called a "cold bitch," accused of infidelity, of being vapid; criticized by feminists and tabloids alike
- Navidson, by contrast, also includes evidence of *his own* failings in the film — some critics think he went too far

### Two Key Moments of Emotional Subtext

**1. The Jewelry Box / Hairbrush**
- Navidson unpacks Karen's jewelry box, looks at something inside the camera can't see, then starts cleaning her old hairbrush
- Karen catches him: *"Give me that. Just you watch, one day I'll go bald, then won't you be sorry you threw that away."*
- *"No,"* he replies. Sarcastic on surface, but his *preserving* of her things tells the opposite story.

**2. Karen Watching for Navidson**
- Karen sits with the kids doing a candle-making craft but compulsively looks out the window every 100 seconds
- When Navidson's car pulls in, she leaps up — then *forces herself back*, playing it cool
- When they meet: *"Hey, the water heater's on the fritz."* — She masks her relief completely.

---

## The Name Delial

Karen's private journal entries (recorded on Hi 8 Navidson gave her) reveal the deeper wound:
> *"He mentioned Delial again... Last night I wasn't sleeping well... He was talking in his sleep: 'Delial.' Just like that. Out of the blue."*

- **Delial** is a name Navidson mutters in his sleep and occasionally aloud
- Karen and Navidson's friends have asked — no one gets an explanation
- No one knows who Delial is. Chapter XIX reportedly deals with this exclusively.

---

## Johnny Truant's Footnote (FN 18) — Key

A long, sprawling footnote in which Johnny describes a night with Lude picking up girls at a bar using an entirely fabricated story about being a "Pit Boxer" — a bare-knuckle fight coordinator in Houston who escaped a Russian sea captain's wrath using exotic birds (Birds of Paradise).

The story is pure fiction; Johnny admits it:
> *"Just sorta doesn't sit right with me. It's like there's something else, something beyond it all, a greater story still looming in the twilight, which for some reason I'm unable to see."*

He then pivots to the cold shower he had that morning — noting that Zampanô only wrote "heater" and **he added the word "water"**. This is an open confession that he has altered the text. The editorial intrusion ends with Johnny clearly rattled by emotions he can't explain.

## Key Themes

- **The documentary within the documentary**: Navidson's film is also about his marriage — but the house took over
- **Suppressed feeling**: Both Karen and Navidson are deeply emotional people who perform indifference
- **The pastoral as illusion**: Every frame radiates optimism Navidson knows is about to end (nostalgia shapes the final cut)
- **The female gaze**: Karen's Hi 8 journal is a confessional layer within the film

---

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

- Johnny admits adding "water" to Zampanô's text — *what else has he added or altered?*
- Daisy's nipped finger, the faulty heater — these small domestic accidents feel mundane but form a pattern of the house as subtly hostile
- Karen's claustrophobia is not mentioned yet — but her freezing at thresholds and discomfort begin here
- **Delial** — this name will be important. Why does Navidson speak it in his sleep?
- Chad's observation: *"It's like something's waiting"* — first child's-eye warning in the book
