## What This Chapter Is

Chapter IX is the labyrinth chapter in both form and content. Zampanô uses the Minotaur myth, Derrida's theory of structure, and the etymological roots of the word "labyrinth" itself to frame what is happening inside the Navidson house — while the house, during this chapter, proves itself a labyrinth in the most catastrophic sense. Exploration #4's return journey collapses into chaos: supplies are destroyed, Holloway loses his mind, and he shoots Wax. The chapter ends with Jed and a bleeding Wax hiding in the dark from their own team leader as Holloway screams somewhere in the black.

## Zampanô's Academic Frame: The Labyrinth Essay

Zampanô opens with an extended scholarly analysis of labyrinths before returning to the Navidson footage:

### The Minotaur Reread
Zampanô proposes that King Minos did not build the Knossos labyrinth to imprison a monster — he built it to hide a deformed child. The "Minotaur" (man with a bull's head = a man with a hideous face) was his own son, and the Athenian youths "fed" to him largely starved in the maze's complexity rather than being killed by any beast. The labyrinth was a structure of **repression**, designed to conceal what could not be accepted.

This reading is illustrated through Taggert Chiclitz's obscure 1972 play *The Minotaur*, in which Minos secretly loves his imprisoned son, sends convicted criminals into the maze (pretending they are sacrificial youths), and is ultimately destroyed by grief when Theseus hacks the Minotaur apart without understanding what he has killed.

### Derrida and the Centerless Structure
Zampanô quotes Jacques Derrida (*Structure, Sign, and Play*):

> *"The center is at the center of the totality, and yet, since the center does not belong to the totality, the totality has its center elsewhere. The center is not the center."*

The house has no center. No one ever sees the whole. Every path is singular and applicable only to whoever walks it at that moment. All solutions are necessarily personal.

### Doob's Labyrinth Paradox
Penelope Reed Doob distinguishes between *maze-treaders* (confused, vision blocked) and *maze-viewers* (those who see the pattern whole from outside). The house breaks this dichotomy entirely: no one ever sees it whole. There is no outside view. Comprehension must always be derived from within.

### The Etymology of "Labyrinth"
The Latin *labor* (toil, difficulty) is cognate with *labi* (to slip or slide backwards). A labyrinth demands constant effort simply to avoid falling — to stop is to fail. Hugh of Saint Victor contrasts the labyrinth (which contains work) with Noah's Ark (which contains rest).

### The House's Constants
A footnote lists what the house maintains regardless of wall-shifts:
- Temperature: 32°F ±8
- Light: **absent**
- Silence: **complete** *(except for the growl)*
- Air Movement: **none**
- True North: **does not exist**

## The Navidson Record: Exploration #4 — The Return

### Day 5: The Long Climb Begins
Holloway's team — Holloway Roberts, Jed Leeder, and Wax Hook — finally begins the trek back up the Spiral Staircase. The staircase's diameter has widened to more than **750 feet**. The distances they expected to cover in 8 hours take 12. Holloway grows increasingly "atrabilious" (melancholic, darkly obsessive) while Jed and Wax remain focused on getting home.

### Day 6: Destroyed Caches
On the sixth day of the return, Holloway finds one of their one-foot neon markers **badly mauled** — fabric torn, as if by some unimaginable claw. The next cache has been **gutted**: only fragments of the plastic water jug and scattered PowerBar pieces remain. Fuel has vanished entirely.

Wax: *"That's nice."*
Jed: *"Holy shit!"*

Holloway's reaction is not analytical — it is a hunter's response. He loads his **Weatherby 300 magnum** with six 180-grain rounds and begins stalking doorways. For the first time in the house, Holloway looks almost happy: something concrete to pursue. He chases the growl down branching corridors, no longer laying fishing line or neon markers, barely stopping to rest.

Jed and Wax understand what Holloway does not: they are moving farther from the staircase without getting closer to the sound. They insist on turning back. Holloway first stalls, then resorts to screaming abuse at them — *"fucking pussies," "cowards," "come-guzzling shit-eating cunts"* — before storming off alone into the blackness.

### Day 7: Holloway Is Gone
Jed and Wax wait. They make a brief search. They find nothing — no neon markers, no fishing line. Holloway has run off **blind** into the dark.

They camp. In the morning there is still no sign of him. They make the only decision they can: go back without him.

### The Shooting
As Jed and Wax climb, they discover every neon marker they left has been **torn apart** — worse the higher they go. Wax notices his buttons are missing, strips of velcro have fallen off his parka, his shoelaces have shredded. Even his pack frame has crumbled. The house is consuming their gear.

They reach a cache. The food and fuel are gone. The water jug is intact.

Wax reaches for a second drink.

A rifle shot drops him to the floor. **Blood gushes from his left armpit.**

Holloway emerges from the dark corridor holding his weapon. He seems as confused as he is present:

> *"How the hell did I get here? I thought it was that, that thing. Fuck. It was that thing. I'm sure of it. That awful fucking… fuck."*

Jed snaps him into helping treat the wound — gauze, ace bandages, disinfectant, painkillers. Wax's agony only partly subsides.

Then Jed tells Holloway what they'll have to do to carry Wax out.

> *"Are you crazy? I can't go back now. I just shot someone."*

Holloway's concern is not Wax — it is himself. Prison. Losing everything. He sits down. He mumbles. Jed begs. Holloway stands, walks to the edge of the light, and disappears again into the black.

### The Pursuit
Jed has barely moved Wax when **two bullets smash into a nearby wall**. Holloway's helmet light is visible across the stairway. He is hunting them.

Jed kills his flashlight and drags Wax into a narrow hallway branching off the staircase. He moves through a series of tiny rooms, clicking his light on and off in brief flashes to navigate, another shot answering each burst of light. He pushes until his lungs give out.

Jed slides to the floor, turns off the light, and starts to sob.

### Jed's Testimony
At 3:31 A.M., Jed turns the camera on himself and speaks directly into it. He recounts the whole sequence — Holloway's break, the shooting, the escape. He admits he no longer knows where they are:

> *"So much for my sense of direction. I've spent the last hour looking for a way back to the staircase. No luck. The radio is useless. If help doesn't come soon, he'll die. I'll die."*

In the frame, barely visible, we see Jed's fist **rapping against the floor** — which produces the same timbre as the knocking heard in the living room at the end of Chapter VIII. But as Alan P. Winnett notes, the pattern does not resemble SOS. The house appears to have **translated** Jed's random knocking into the distress signal Navidson heard. Or, as Maria Hulbert counters: by day 8, the absence itself *was already the distress signal*.

### Wax's Condition
Wax tries to smile for the camera. He asks for water seconds after drinking. Jed — recognizing shock — raises Wax's legs, wraps him in a solar blanket, and keeps talking, keeps promising, keeps telling jokes.

Then Holloway's screaming finds them through the walls. Not words anymore. A rabid, animal sound. Jed whispers that at least he's far off.

Neither of them is comforted.

## Zampanô's Historical Parallels

Zampanô uses two historical expeditions to frame Holloway's collapse:

### Magellan and the Mutiny (1520)
During Magellan's circumnavigation, winter conditions, scarce stores, and anxiety about the future triggered a mutiny on April 1, 1520. Magellan suppressed it with tactical precision and lethal force: the mutineer Quesada was executed by his own servant Molino, who accepted clemency in exchange for killing his master. The mutineers underestimated Magellan. That misjudgment cost them their lives.

Like Magellan, Holloway faced men who wanted to turn back. Unlike Magellan, Holloway's course was doomed — so Zampanô turns to the second parallel.

### Henry Hudson and the Shallop (1611)
Hudson's fourth voyage ended in the bay bearing his name, frozen in ice, provisions running out. His crew mutinied, forced Hudson, his son, and seven others into a small boat with no food or water. They were never seen again — *"lost in a labyrinth without end."*

Like Hudson, Holloway resisted men who wanted to turn back. Unlike Hudson, Holloway chose to go into that labyrinth. Willingly. Alone.

## Johnny Truant's Footnotes — Key Content

### FN Opening — Johnny's Panic Attack
Johnny can barely leave his apartment. Reaching for the door latch makes him physically ill — chest tightness, racing pulse. He describes it with characteristic self-mockery: he's laughing at his own irrational fear while simultaneously being "absolutely terrified." He mutters a string of profanities and charges out.

He was fine. He made it to Tatiana's.

### FN — Tatiana's Apartment *(explicit)*
Tatiana, a former scribe of Zampanô's, provides Johnny with Zampanô's old library request lists — revealing how the old man obscured his secondary sources. The footnote then turns explicit: Johnny describes a sexual encounter with Tatiana in graphic detail. He notes afterward that "getting off" seemed to temporarily relieve the dread that had been consuming him. Thumper was right. He just needed to get out of the house.

### FN 141 — Johnny Feels Better, Then Goes Out
Empowered by his realization, Johnny makes a round of calls (Tatiana not home, Amber's machine, Lude). He and Lude end up at a bar called Red, where Johnny spots a woman named Natasha — whom he describes as "Tolstoy's prophecy brought to life." Lude, attempting to help, claims a piece of Zampanô's writing (about love at first sight) was written by Johnny for her. Natasha rejects the offer and returns the poem. Johnny flees. He continues bar-hopping, ends up drunk, picks up an Australian woman, and spends the night with her — also described in explicit detail.

The footnote ends with a rueful self-assessment: *"Yeah I know, the dots here don't really connect."*

### FN 164 — The Ghost in the Dining Hall
A long, elegiac footnote in which Johnny recalls a moment at boarding school: sitting in a noisy dining hall, he heard a girl's voice whisper his name — twice. The sound had been carried perfectly by the domed ceiling from the far side of the room. He could never find the girl. The footnote expands into a meditation on Johnny's scars (burn scars from a dropped pan of corn oil at age 4, plus others), his mother (whom he told people was dead to simplify his life), and a recurring daydream — a girl with eyes like the northern sky.

The footnote concludes with Ashley: a woman he finally connects with in Venice, who sits on a hammock with him, has sex with him in silence, and then explains their entire prior history (meeting in Texas, a kiss, years of haunting). Johnny's response: *"I've never been to Texas."*

### FN — Knocking and the Ghost
Johnny explicitly connects the knocking Jed produces in the house to the ghost story — the sound triggered the memory. The whole extended boarding school reverie was born from the image of Jed's fist on the floor, carrying through walls.

## Key Themes

- **The labyrinth as repression**: Minos built it to hide what he could not accept. Holloway is building his own internal one — the house is just the architecture of it.
- **No map is possible**: The house prohibits mapmaking. Every solution is singular. No god, no Other, no one who hasn't been there can help you find a way out.
- **Darkness consumes reason**: Holloway's obsession with hunting something real is the mind's response to the unbearable formlessness of the dark. A monster to pursue is better than nothing to pursue.
- **The house translates**: Jed's random knocking becomes SOS by the time it reaches the living room. The house processes distress into legible signal — or the absence of signal was always already the message.
- **Johnny's panic mirrors the labyrinth**: He can barely leave his apartment. The house in the text is bleeding into his life. His footnotes are themselves a maze — branching, self-consuming, always promising resolution.

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

- Holloway says *"I thought it was that thing"* when he shoots Wax — he was hunting the growl's source and fired at movement in the dark. He is not a murderer yet. But what he does next — abandoning Wax, then firing at Jed and Wax to keep them from leaving — edges toward it.
- The house destroys gear (buttons, velcro, pack frames) in ways that defy physical explanation. It is expurgating the team.
- Wax's thirst, seconds after drinking water, is a classic sign of shock. Jed knows this. The question is whether they survive long enough for Navidson to reach them.
- Johnny's scars: the corn oil story is presented as "not nearly as dramatic" as other explanations he'd offered. The other scars — "too many to start babbling on about" — remain unexplained.
