# House of Leaves — Chapter VI Recap

**Epigraphs:**
- Ernest Becker on animals lacking self-consciousness and the knowledge of death
- Christian Norberg-Schulz: *"While the pragmatic space of animals is a function of inborn instincts, man has to learn what orientation he needs in order to act."*

---

## What This Chapter Is

Chapter VI is the shortest chapter so far — almost entirely a meditation on the **animals' relationship to the house**, anchored by a single strange event: on August 11, 1990, both pets enter the hallway and somehow exit into the backyard with no explanation. The chapter ends by introducing **Holloway Roberts**, and Johnny's footnote becomes one of the most disturbing entries yet.

---

## The Pets at Chapter's End (Framing)

Zampanô opens with their *final* states — a flash-forward:
- **Hillary** (the Siberian husky): By the end of the film, he's no longer a puppy. Something watchful has taken up residence in his eyes. He growls at strangers. *Changed* by the house.
- **Mallory** (the tabby cat): **Vanishes completely.** No explanation is ever given. His disappearance is never revisited by Navidson or Zampanô.
- *"The house played a very small part in both their histories."* (Deliberately ambiguous — a small part that permanently altered one and erased the other.)

---

## The Animal Incident — August 11, 1990

**Date:** Saturday morning, approximately one week after Navidson's **secret exploration** of the hallway (which we have not yet been shown).

### The Setup
- Saturday morning cartoons on the kitchen TV; Chad and Daisy eating breakfast
- Karen is **outside**, smoking, talking to Audrie McCullogh on the phone — ranting about Feng Shui failing her: *"No matter how many ceramic turtles or wooden ducks, goldfish, celestial dragons, or bronze lions I put in this goddamn house, it still keeps throwing off this awful energy. I need to find a psychic. Or an exorcist. Or a really good real-estate agent."*
- Tom and Navidson are in the living room taking **still shots of the hallway using a strobe**

### The Event
- Mallory and Hillary begin a chase routine (not unusual)
- After racing over the sofa, **both animals run straight down the hallway and disappear into the darkness**
- An instant later: barking from *outside*, followed by Karen shouting at Navidson about letting the animals out (they were supposed to stay in)
- Hillary is in the backyard, howling triumphantly. Mallory is up a tree.
- **They went into the hallway and came out in the backyard.** No explanation is ever given for how.

### What the Critics Say (and Don't Say)
- Bernard Porch's 4,000-page treatise on the film devotes *one third of a sentence* to the event: *"(strange how the house won't support the presence of animals)"*
- Mary Widmunt: *"So what's the deal with the pets?"*
- Navidson himself — the consummate investigator — **never revisits the subject**

> *"Who knows what might have been discovered if he had."*

## Holloway Arrives

Immediately after the animal incident, **Holloway Roberts** arrives. Any inquiry into the animal phenomenon is abandoned in favor of human exploration.

This is the first time Holloway is placed *at the house*. (We have seen him lost inside it in "Exploration #4" from Chapter I — the chapter plays with time, showing us his end before his beginning.)

## Johnny Truant's Footnote (FN 82) — Most Disturbing Yet

This footnote is the longest and strangest in the book so far. Johnny is **drunk and high** while writing it — but that doesn't fully explain what it contains.

### The Thumper Update
- Thumper came into the Shop
- Johnny had written her a poem (*"with all that coastal norths and August-sun scent-of-pine-trees stuff"* — the same language from his Chapter V fantasies about her)
- He put it in an envelope and handed it to her without a word
- She opened it, read it, **laughed**. His boss grabbed it and winced: *"Now look who's the dumb mutant."*
- Thumper had him hang up her flip-flops and sweatpants, stretched out on the tattoo chair — **business as usual**
- Johnny hid in the back of the Shop, afraid to go too far back because of the storeroom
- Just before leaving, Thumper handed him her card: *"Call me some time. You're cute."*
- His life *"instantly changed. I thought."*
- He waited. Re-considered. Postponed. At 3:22am, called the number. It's a **beeper**. He punched in his number.
- An hour passed. He started drinking. **She never called back.**

### The Drift — Cats, Death, and the Thing in the Dark

From the Thumper disappointment, Johnny spirals into a long, winding, alcohol-and-grief-fueled meditation:

- He envies Hillary and Mallory for their animal unconsciousness of death
- He thinks about Zampanô's 80+ cats in the courtyard — *"so many shades slinking out from under that dusty place like years, his years"*
- The meditation on cats becomes language-drunk poetry about perception, play, and the obliteration of self in sensation
- It detours into something dark: Johnny hints at time spent in Ohio, under the care of a man *"with a beard rougher than horse hide and hands harder than horn, who called me beast because I was his boy though he wasn't my father"* — a **foster caretaker** who was apparently abusive. He refuses to go further: *"which is another story, another place I'm here to avoid"*

### One of Zampanô's Female Readers — The Creature
A woman who read to Zampanô told Johnny a story she had never wanted to share:
- Left Zampanô's apartment at nightfall, disoriented by hours of his speech on *"comfort, death and legend... mothers & daughters and birds & bees"*
- Walking back to her car (in Santa Cruz, California — it was pouring rain there, though not on Franklin Ave), she noticed *"the unnatural heaviness of a shadow slipping free from the burnt dusk, though not a shadow at all"*
- She saw *"an enormous creature trespassing on the curve of a Northern California night"* — and panicked into a nearby bar
- The next morning, hangover: only a fleeting memory of *"something white with ropes of sea smoke and one terrifying flash of blue"*
- *"More than she could usually share even if — which she wouldn't share — she knew it still wasn't even the half of it"*

### Johnny's Ending
The footnote closes with Johnny watching Zampanô's imagined courtyard go dark from the window of his room:

> *"Everything whimsical has left."*
> *"But the old man is not there, and the cats are all gone."*
> *"Something else has taken their place. Something I am unable to see. Waiting."*
> *"I'm afraid."*
> *"It is hungry. It is immortal."*
> *"Worse, it knows nothing of whim."*

## Key Themes

- **Animals as early warning system**: They sense what humans rationalize away; their complete avoidance/expulsion from the hallway is significant — the house is not for them
- **Human self-consciousness as burden**: Becker's epigraph — animals live without knowing death; humans cannot. The house exploits that knowledge.
- **Repetition and echo (continued)**: Zampanô still not commenting on the animal event mirrors Navidson's own silence on it — both men avoid what they cannot explain
- **The creature outside the house**: Johnny's report of the reader's sighting suggests the entity is not *contained* inside the house — it may move

---

## Contradictions / Things to Watch

- **Navidson's secret exploration** is mentioned here as having already happened (a week before August 11) — but we have not seen it. He *broke his promise to Karen* between Chapters V and VI. This will presumably be shown.
- The animals went into the hallway and came out in the backyard — the hallway and the backyard are somehow connected spatially, even though the house is surrounded by yard. This implies the hallway's geography is not fixed or rational.
- Mallory's permanent disappearance (stated at the chapter's opening as a future fact) — was it the house? The creature?
- The creature seen by Zampanô's reader: *white, with ropes of sea smoke, a flash of blue* — something physical, enormous, and outdoor. Not the same as the dark presence in the hallway. Or is it?
- Johnny's abusive foster caretaker in Ohio — another layer of his backstory, hinting his scars are from this man, not a Japanese cult
- **FN 82 ends with Johnny claiming to be afraid of the thing he cannot see in the courtyard** — he is now mirroring not just Zampanô's behaviors but Zampanô's *final days*
